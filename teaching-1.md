# Teaching Philosophy: Why Teaching Speaks to Me

## Teaching as a Natural Orientation

**Teaching Philosophy: Why Teaching Speaks to Me** is not only the title of this piece, but also a way of describing how I orient myself towards academic life. Teaching is something that speaks to me in a quiet but persistent way. As we go along through academic and professional experience, it becomes natural for me to reflect on what might have been without those who take time to explain, guide, and share knowledge.

Matter-of-factly, I think teaching is essentially an act of knowledge transfer — but it is never only technical. It is put into practice not just by a sense of duty, but out of love and sharing. That distinction matters to me: teaching is not simply what one does, but how one relates to others in the process of learning.

I have always loved reading and numbers, and it was therefore a natural fit for me to move towards teaching roles, particularly in analytical and computational subjects. Over time, this has developed into a consistent way of thinking rather than a single career choice.

> Teaching, for me, is less about performance and more about presence — being attentive to how others understand, and adjusting accordingly.

## Beyond Delivery

Teaching is not simply about delivering content. It is about helping students build a richer learning experience, where ideas are not only understood but interrogated, debated, and applied.

What is put into practice as the result of teaching matters to me deeply. I am interested in how students develop judgement and discernment, especially when faced with perplexing problems that do not have immediate or obvious answers.

In this sense, teaching is not matter-of-factly linear. It is iterative. It evolves as students respond, question, and sometimes challenge what is being presented.

## Reflection

Teaching comes naturally to me, not because I assume ease, but because I enjoy working with others and sharing knowledge in a structured yet human way. It sits at the intersection of organisation, curiosity, and care.

I do not see teaching as separate from learning. As we go along, both are continuously shaping each other.
