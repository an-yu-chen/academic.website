# ✦ Professional Development and Communication ✦

> *Teaching Series IV — Growth as an educator through training, communication, and reflection*

---

## Continuous Improvement in Teaching Practice

I have undertaken a range of relevant training and development activities that have shaped my approach to teaching. I joined the AMBS PhD pool and acquired a wide set of tutoring resources and skills through structured workshops and peer learning environments.

These experiences reinforced for me that teaching is not static. It evolves continuously through exposure, reflection, and practice.

---

## Learning from Others

A significant part of my development came from observing and learning from experienced educators.

I learned a great deal from Chris Godfrey in terms of becoming a better teaching assistant both inside and outside the classroom. I also learned from Alex Gunz about effective teaching approaches, particularly in how to help students and audiences understand complex content through structure, narrative, and clarity.

There were also important lessons in teaching design — particularly how scheme and narrative skills shape the way ideas are received and understood.

---

## Communication and Confidence

In addition, I took part in workshops at MIOIR on public speaking with Ed Darling, a communication coach and founder of Project Charisma.

That experience helped me become much more confident in my own skin and in interacting with others. It also made me more aware that teaching is not only about knowledge, but about presence, tone, and clarity of communication.

MIOIR is a community of global pioneers, and being part of that environment encouraged me to think outside the box and connect the dots across disciplines and perspectives.

---

## Reflection

In spite of this structured development, I find that teaching is still something that feels natural to me. I am organised, self-motivated, and comfortable working with others in collaborative environments.

As I go along, I continue to refine how I communicate ideas — not just for accuracy, but for clarity, engagement, and accessibility.
