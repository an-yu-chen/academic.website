    <h2>Responsibility in Knowledge Gatekeeping</h2>

    <p>
      My experience as a reviewer for the <em>Journal of Information Science</em>, <em>Journal of Management and Governance</em>, and <em>Humanities and Social Sciences Communications</em> has shaped my understanding of academic responsibility from another angle.
    </p>

    <p>
      Reviewing is often invisible work, yet it plays a central role in shaping scholarly communication. The whole thing about peer review is trust — trust in fairness, judgement, and academic integrity.
    </p>

    <p>
      I strive to approach reviewing with fairness, curiosity, and constructive feedback. I always respond to the work with curiosity and excitement, even when identifying limitations or weaknesses.
    </p>

    <p class="quote">
      A good reviewer does not close doors; they open better questions.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="journey-section">
    <h2>What Reviewing Taught Me</h2>

    <p>
      There is a quiet responsibility here: offering critique while maintaining respect. As one senior editor once remarked in a workshop I attended, this idea stayed with me deeply. I took this information on board because it reflects the kind of academic culture I value.
    </p>

    <p>
      That is what keeps me going — the belief that careful feedback can help shape stronger, more inclusive research. Then again, we can think about reviewing not only as evaluation, but as mentorship.
    </p>

    <p>
      Courage and strength often come from recognising that small unseen contributions can still make a remarkable difference.
    </p>
