    <p>From Taiwan to Manchester — a journey in economics, innovation, and the science of science</p>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="card">
      <h2>My Academic Journey</h2>

      <p>
        Four years rolled by, and my PhD journey has now reached one of its most meaningful milestones. 
        Looking back, I can clearly see how much I have grown—not only as a researcher, but also as a person. 
        I have made real progress, and that progress came from persistence, reflection, and an enduring curiosity about how knowledge shapes innovation.
      </p>

      <p>
        I have always loved numbers and logic. Even from an early stage, I liked solving problems and I liked getting things done on time. 
        Data has become part of my life, and my attention on this seldom wavered. 
        I followed this keenly for many years because I believed that understanding data means understanding how societies progress.
      </p>

      <p>
        When I was an undergraduate student at <strong>National Tsing Hua University</strong>, I studied Economics and worked very hard, graduating with a GPA of 3.7. 
        During that period, I became increasingly interested in innovation, industrial development, and economic transformation. 
        Schumpeter’s ideas on innovation and entrepreneurship made my mind full of ideas and encouraged me to think about something more ambitious.
      </p>

      <p>
        After completing my BA in Economics, I transferred my academic focus to Technology Management for my Master’s degree at the Institute of Technology Management, also at National Tsing Hua University. 
        I was keen to make a start in a field where economics, management, and technological change could meet. 
        Soon after, I learnt to use bibliometric methods, research databases, and empirical tools for innovation studies. 
        It meant that I began to understand how scientific knowledge travels into industry and how policy can shape that process.
      </p>

      <p>
        In 2015, I received a DAAD scholarship from the German Academic Exchange Service and joined Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg for a three-month internship on 
        <strong>Conceive Product with the Concept of Human-Centred and Emotional Design</strong>. 
        I remained there for some time, and this experience deepened my understanding of integrated design engineering. 
        It also gave me wisdom and discernment about international collaboration and interdisciplinary research.
      </p>

      <p>
        After receiving my Master’s degree, I worked at <strong>Booking.com</strong> as a Customer Service Executive. 
        What I did in my last job taught me how to work efficiently in an international environment and how to manage complex systems and people with patience. 
        It also strengthened my confidence in communication and problem-solving. 
        I am increasingly asserting myself with great confidence because of those experiences.
      </p>

      <p>
        From 2017 to 2020, I returned to academia and became a Student Research Assistant at <strong>National Taiwan University</strong>. 
        I also worked as a Teaching Assistant for undergraduate Business Management courses and assisted the journal editor of the 
        <strong>Taiwan Hsinchu Industry and Management Forum</strong>. 
        During this period, I worked on government projects involving science and technology policy evaluation for the Ministry of Science and Technology in Taiwan.
      </p>

      <p>
        I also developed strong technical skills in Python, R, SQL, Web of Science, Scopus, USPTO, and the EU Community Innovation Survey. 
        As time went on, I became more and more devoted to research policy, technology management, and innovation studies. 
        My work in publication analysis and patent databases made me realise that I wanted to contribute something more substantial to the field.
      </p>

      <p>
        In 2018, I presented my first academic conference paper at PICMET in Honolulu, Hawaii, on technological entrepreneurship and new product development. 
        This was a major highlight of my early academic life. 
        I also received a Ministry of Science and Technology grant, which further encouraged me to continue.
      </p>

      <p>
        Why I chose Manchester to do my PhD was simple: it is the birthplace of the Industrial Revolution. 
        Manchester is a city built on ideas, transformation, and progress. 
        It is constituted with vibrant communities, and it remains at the forefront of science, policy, and innovation. 
        It is still going strong today, with an exciting future of continued progress.
      </p>

      <p>
        At the <strong>Manchester Institute of Innovation Research (MIOIR)</strong> and 
        <strong>Alliance Manchester Business School (AMBS)</strong>, I became part of a world-leading research environment. 
        I am inspired by the world-leading minds around me, and in Manchester I benefit greatly from links to industry, policy institutions, and outstanding scholars. 
        Just the friends I made—from people working in different and promising areas—have shaped me enormously.
      </p>

      <p>
        My PhD focused on the contribution of scientific knowledge to green technology development. 
        Through bibliometric analysis, patent studies, and science-policy research, I examined how scientific foundations drive innovation in green technologies. 
        I plucked up the courage to ask bigger questions and to pursue answers that could contribute to sustainability transitions and science policy.
      </p>

      <p>
        I am immensely proud to have completed my PhD and to have continued as a Research Associate in Science of Science at the University of Manchester. 
        I am happy to have been part of projects involving EPO datasets, OpenAlex, Scopus, and large-scale doctoral thesis analysis. 
        Nearly every stage of this work reminded me that research is not only about discovery, but also about responsibility.
      </p>

      <p>
        Outside academia, I enjoy wellness activities to boost the mind and maintain balance. 
        I like to devise a little pastime—something to keep my mind active and help ideas settle naturally. 
        Whether through reflection, reading, or long walks, these moments help me to flourish.
      </p>

      <p>
        Today, I feel I am shining brighter than ever. 
        I remain committed to economics of innovation, knowledge management, green technologies, and the science of science. 
        I believe the future belongs to those who can connect knowledge with purpose, and I hope to continue contributing to that future with clarity, discipline, and curiosity.
      </p>

      <div class="quote-box">
        “Research is not simply about finding answers. It is about learning how to ask better questions.”
      </div>

    </div>
