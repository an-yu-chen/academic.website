  <div class="page-title">
    <h1>Autobiography</h1>
    <p>
      A journey shaped by economics, innovation, research, and a lasting belief in the power of knowledge.
    </p>
  </div>

  <!-- TWO MAPS -->
   
  <!-- MAIN WRITING -->
  <div class="bio-card">
    <p>
      I was born in Taiwan, where my academic journey first began.
      Growing up there gave me a strong appreciation for education, discipline,
      and persistence. Taiwan’s dynamic environment of technology, industry,
      and innovation shaped my early curiosity about economics and development.
      It was there that I first learned how closely knowledge, policy, and
      progress are connected.
    </p>
    <p>
      I have always loved numbers and logic. Even from an early stage, I liked solving
      problems, and I liked getting things done on time. Data has become part of my life,
      and my attention on this seldom wavered. I followed this keenly for many years
      because I believed that understanding data means understanding how societies progress.
    </p>
    <!-- TAIWAN MAP INSIDE STORY -->
    <div class="map-card" style="margin: 40px 0;">
      <h2>My Birthplace - Taiwan</h2>
      <div id="taiwan-map" class="map-box"></div>
    </div>
    <p>
      When I was an undergraduate student at National Tsing Hua University in Taiwan,
      I studied Economics and worked very hard, graduating with a GPA of 3.7.
      During that period, I became increasingly interested in innovation, industrial
      development, and economic transformation. Schumpeter’s ideas on innovation and
      entrepreneurship made my mind full of ideas and encouraged me to think about
      something more ambitious.
    </p>

    <p>
      After completing my BA in Economics, I transferred my academic focus to
      Technology Management for my Master’s degree at the Institute of Technology
      Management, also at National Tsing Hua University. I was keen to make a start
      in a field where economics, management, and technological change could meet.
      Soon after, I learnt to use bibliometric methods, research databases, and
      empirical tools for innovation studies. It meant that I began to understand
      how scientific knowledge travels into industry and how policy can shape that process.
    </p>

    <p>
      In 2015, I received a DAAD scholarship from the German Academic Exchange Service
      and joined Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg for a three-month internship
      on Conceive Product with the Concept of Human-Centred and Emotional Design.
      I remained there for some time, and this experience deepened my understanding
      of integrated design engineering. It also gave me wisdom and discernment about
      international collaboration and interdisciplinary research.
    </p>

    <p>
      After receiving my Master’s degree, I worked at Booking.com as a Customer Service
      Executive. What I did in my last job taught me how to work efficiently in an
      international environment and how to manage complex systems and people with patience.
      It also strengthened my confidence in communication and problem-solving.
      I am increasingly asserting myself with great confidence because of those experiences.
    </p>

    <p>
      From 2017 to 2020, I returned to academia and became a Student Research Assistant
      at National Taiwan University. I also worked as a Teaching Assistant for
      undergraduate Business Management courses and assisted the journal editor of the
      Taiwan Hsinchu Industry and Management Forum. During this period, I worked on
      government projects involving science and technology policy evaluation for the
      Ministry of Science and Technology in Taiwan.
    </p>

    <p>
      I also developed strong technical skills in Python, R, SQL, Web of Science,
      Scopus, USPTO, and the EU Community Innovation Survey. As time went on,
      I became more and more devoted to research policy, technology management,
      and innovation studies. My work in publication analysis and patent databases
      made me realise that I wanted to contribute something more substantial to the field.
    </p>

    <p>
      In 2018, I presented my first academic conference paper at PICMET in Honolulu,
      Hawaii, on technological entrepreneurship and new product development.
      This was a major highlight of my early academic life. I also received a
      Ministry of Science and Technology grant, which further encouraged me to continue.
    </p>

    <p>
      Why I chose Manchester to do my PhD was simple: it is the birthplace of the
      Industrial Revolution. Manchester is a city built on ideas, transformation,
      and progress. It is constituted with vibrant communities, and it remains at
      the forefront of science, policy, and innovation. It is still going strong today,
      with an exciting future of continued progress.
    </p>

    <p>
      At the Manchester Institute of Innovation Research (MIOIR) and Alliance Manchester
      Business School (AMBS), I became part of a world-leading research environment.
      I am inspired by the world-leading minds around me, and in Manchester I benefit
      greatly from links to industry, policy institutions, and outstanding scholars.
      Just the friends I made—from people working in different and promising areas—
      have shaped me enormously.
    </p>
    <!-- UK MAP INSIDE STORY -->
    <div class="map-card" style="margin: 40px 0;">
      <h2>United Kingdom, where I studied my PhD</h2>
      <div id="uk-map" class="map-box"></div>
    </div>
    <p>
      Four years rolled by, and my PhD journey reached one of its most meaningful
      milestones. My thesis explored the contribution of scientific knowledge to
      green technology development. Through bibliometric analysis, patent studies,
      and science-policy research, I examined how scientific foundations drive
      innovation in green technologies.
    </p>

    <p>
      I plucked up the courage to ask bigger questions and to pursue answers that
      could contribute to sustainability transitions and science policy.
      I have made real progress, and that progress came from persistence,
      reflection, and an enduring curiosity about how knowledge shapes innovation.
    </p>

    <p>
      I am immensely proud to have completed my PhD and to have continued as a
      Research Associate in Science of Science at the University of Manchester.
      I am happy to have been part of projects involving EPO datasets, OpenAlex,
      Scopus, and large-scale doctoral thesis analysis. Nearly every stage of this
      work reminded me that research is not only about discovery, but also about responsibility.
    </p>

    <p>
      Outside academia, I enjoy wellness activities to boost the mind and maintain
      balance. I like to devise a little pastime—something to keep my mind active
      and help ideas settle naturally. Whether through reflection, reading, or long walks,
      these moments help me to flourish.
    </p>

    <div class="quote-line">
      “The roots of education are bitter, but the fruit is sweet.”~ Aristotle
    </div>

    <p>
      Today, I feel I am shining brighter than ever. I remain committed to
      economics of innovation, knowledge management, policy planning,
      management of science and technology, green technologies, and the science of science.
      I believe the future belongs to those who can connect knowledge with purpose,
      and I hope to continue contributing to that future with clarity,
      discipline, and curiosity.
    </p>
    <p>
  I am also deeply grateful for the people I have met through conferences,
  workshops, and academic exchanges over the years. These encounters have
  broadened my perspective, encouraged meaningful conversations, and reminded
  me that research is strengthened through collaboration.
</p>
