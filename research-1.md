# AI, Knowledge Systems and Research Transformation

## Why research into AI?

Why research into AI? The real reason was never simply technological novelty, but a broader concern with how knowledge systems reorganise themselves under conditions of acceleration and complexity.

The one thing is clear: artificial intelligence is no longer external to scientific practice. It is increasingly embedded within how research questions are formed, how evidence is processed, and how outputs are evaluated (IPCC, 2021, Chapter 3: Human Influence on the Climate System, AR6 WG1. https://doi.org/10.1017/9781009157896.005).

## What does knowledge under transformation actually mean?

In the lights of all that, it becomes necessary to ask how can we use AI to achieve the potential of scientific discovery without reducing the epistemic richness of research itself.

That’s precisely why I’m really interested in the intersection between AI and science policy.

This is something that speaks to the immediate concerns of reproducibility, scale, and interpretability in contemporary research environments (Rolnick et al., 2022, *Tackling Climate Change with Machine Learning*, ACM Computing Surveys, 55(2), 1–96. https://doi.org/10.1145/3485128).

## How is the research landscape shifting?

How that would have been inconceivable forty years ago is now a daily reality in data-driven science.

However, those new technologies make it harder to control, particularly when decision-making becomes increasingly automated.

It’s a mixture of both opportunity and uncertainty.

The real challenge is not whether AI should be used, but how we can structure its use within scientific systems that remain accountable, transparent, and socially responsive.

## AI, Grand Challenges and Climate Transitions

Artificial intelligence matters not only because of its computational capacity, but because it is increasingly embedded within the governance of grand societal challenges, particularly climate change, sustainability transitions, and energy system transformation.

In this sense, AI is not an isolated technological field; it is becoming part of how societies attempt to organise responses to complexity. This includes climate modelling, resource optimisation, and large-scale environmental monitoring systems (Kaack et al., 2022, *Aligning artificial intelligence with climate change mitigation*, Nature Climate Change, 12, 518–527. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-022-01377-7).

The one thing is clear: climate change is not only a scientific problem, but a coordination problem across knowledge systems, policy institutions, and technological infrastructures (IPCC, 2023, *AR6 Synthesis Report*, https://doi.org/10.59327/IPCC/AR6-9789291691647).

## Why does AI become central to sustainability systems?

Can we use AI to achieve its potential in addressing climate-related challenges?

This question is not purely technical. It relates to how data-driven systems reshape decision-making under uncertainty. For instance, machine learning methods are increasingly used in climate prediction, ecological monitoring, and energy demand forecasting (IPCC, 2021, Chapter 4: Future Global Climate Projections, AR6 WG1. https://doi.org/10.1017/9781009157896.006; Kaack et al., 2022).

However, this also introduces a tension: while AI expands analytical capacity, it also increases dependency on opaque systems that are difficult to audit or govern.

That’s precisely why I’m really interested in how AI is positioned within research systems that aim to address sustainability transitions.

## What happens when knowledge systems come under pressure?

Research is no longer only about generating knowledge, but about structuring how knowledge is mobilised under urgency.

Take the world’s greatest challenges—climate change, inequality, and digital transformation. These are not separate domains; they increasingly interact through shared infrastructures of data, computation, and policy.

AI becomes a major challenge of our time, particularly because it requires coordination across scientific, political, and technological systems simultaneously (IPCC, 2023; Kaack et al., 2022).

## Reflection: AI as infrastructure, not just a tool

The real reason AI matters is not simply automation or efficiency.

It is because AI is becoming part of the infrastructure of knowledge itself—shaping what is seen, measured, and acted upon.

However, those new technologies make it harder to control, particularly when they are embedded in high-stakes systems such as climate governance or scientific funding allocation.

And because of that, we need more careful, structured thinking about how AI is governed within research and policy systems.

## Closing reflection

That’s precisely why I’m interested in understanding how research systems evolve under the combined pressure of technological acceleration and climate urgency.

In various issues in between theory and application, the challenge is not only innovation—but alignment between knowledge production and societal need.
