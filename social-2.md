    <h2>Policy, Translation and Responsibility</h2>

    <p>
      In 2015, I joined the Center for Asia Policy (CAP) at National Tsing Hua University in Hsinchu, Taiwan, as an intern. Working under the guidance of Bill (William A. Stanton), this became another formative stage in my understanding of social responsibility in academic and policy contexts.
    </p>

    <p>
      My responsibilities included classifying news related to Asia policy, investigating public policy and international affairs, summarising think tank meetings, and translating materials into English. The implication of that work extended beyond administration — it involved understanding how knowledge travels across institutions and borders.
    </p>

    <p>
      I was quite into understanding how policy narratives are constructed. Leading to a better understanding of how information moves between academic, governmental, and public spaces, I began to appreciate that translation is not merely linguistic — it is interpretive and ethical.
    </p>

    <p class="quote">
      Translation is not only about language — it is about responsibility, context, and trust.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="journey-section">
    <h2>What I Learned</h2>

    <p>
      It also involves dealing with nuance and uncertainty. There were moments when I thought that in itself would not have been sufficient if done mechanically. I had to remind myself that clarity requires responsibility. When one explanation did not work, I tried another tactic.
    </p>

    <p>
      My increasingly thought as I get older is that knowledge production carries responsibility. We are living through challenging times, and how information is framed can either offer hope or deepen confusion. I strive to serve communities by contributing carefully and honestly to this process.
    </p>

    <p>
      At the forefront of my thinking is how that could look like in practice. Collectively we can achieve that, even if immediate results are not always visible.
    </p>
