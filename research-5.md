Gender, Knowledge Production and Innovation Inequality

Let me start with empirical patterns in innovation systems—what do the data actually show when we look at how knowledge production operates in practice? At any rate, what became clear early on was that knowledge production is not neutral.

My research on gender and innovation revealed systematic differences in how scientific knowledge is used within patenting teams.

Gender and Innovation – WIPO Presentation (2025), Alliance Manchester Business School / MIOIR Blog (2026, January 6). https://blogs.manchester.ac.uk/mioir/2026/01/06/reflecting-on-the-global-research-experts-meeting-at-the-world-intellectual-property-organisation-wipo/

What surprised me?

I was impressed by how structural bias emerges even in highly technical domains.

It blew me away how citation behaviours reflect deeper institutional and social dynamics.

Broader implications—what does this mean for how we understand innovation systems?

This is something I’ve committed for a very long time: understanding how inequality is embedded in knowledge systems.

In particular the numerous mechanisms through which gender influences scientific visibility and technological uptake.

This is compounded by the fact that innovation systems are often assumed to be neutral, which they are not.

Reflection—how should we interpret inequality in innovation systems?

That set me to thinking: inequality in innovation is not an externality—it is internal to the system itself.
