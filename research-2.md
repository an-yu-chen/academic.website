# STI Policy, Innovation Systems and Network Dynamics

Let me start with STI policy. The real reason was my growing interest in how institutional frameworks shape scientific collaboration.

STI policy have retained an important function to facilitate interaction between actors in scientific networks, reducing barriers and externalities for knowledge exchange and generation.

Let me ask this directly: what happens when we move beyond linear thinking in policy design?

However, research of policy was often done in linear model that the consideration of dynamic change and interaction of actors tend to be neglected (Flanagan and Uyarra, 2016).

This is something I must emphasise: there is still an overall paucity of research that explains the dynamic knowledge exchange process and how it can reconcile with STI policy tools.

Let me reflect on global and systemic change for a moment.

Mission-oriented innovation systems add another layer to this discussion. Rather than treating innovation policy as neutral or purely facilitative, this perspective emphasises the role of public institutions in actively shaping and directing innovation towards collective goals such as climate neutrality, health resilience, and digital transformation (Mazzucato, 2018). In this sense, innovation systems are not only networked, but purpose-driven, where directionality becomes as important as connectivity.

Throughout the 2000s, in the era of globalisation, scientific activities increasingly aimed for international connections between disciplines.

In the lights of all that, I began to see innovation systems as inherently networked rather than hierarchical.

Let me ask what structural insight actually tells us here.

Numerous studies have shown that policy environments strongly shape knowledge diffusion, but I think it is a mixture of both formal regulation and informal institutional behaviour.

And in various issues in between governance, economics, and research evaluation, the political power was untenable when treated as a static system.

So what does this imply for policy thinking itself?

That set me to thinking: policy is not a framework imposed on science—it is co-produced with science itself.

Which I can see more clearly now through large-scale empirical work.
